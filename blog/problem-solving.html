Javascript Kata


The term "kata" in the context of coding challenges or exercises was originally derived from martial arts. In martial arts, kata refers to a choreographed sequence of movements used for training and practice. The concept of kata was then adapted to programming to describe small coding exercises that developers can practice to improve their skills and problem-solving abilities.


During the week, I engaged in JavaScript kata exercises to boost my skills. I approached the challenges with enthusiasm, employing various techniques and problem-solving strategies. It was an enriching experience that expanded my knowledge of JavaScript.



Problem Solving

I really had a challenging experience with the JavaScript Kata gradebook exercise. It involved transferring array values into object literals, and I struggled for quite some time. I sought help from a facilitator and employed techniques like rubber duck debugging and pseudocode. It was a mix of frustration and enjoyment. Seeing the tests pass brought satisfaction. The exercise taught me patience, perseverance, and the importance of taking breaks and meditating to relieve stress and refresh my mind.



When I elegantly solve a problem.

I may not deem it elegant, but I managed to complete the concatMyArray.js exercise without resorting to the workshop or seeking help from my facilitator. The task involved creating a function to concatenate individual strings within an array into a single string. Employing pseudocode and the rubber duck technique, I conversed with my storm trooper to systematically approach and break down the problem.



How confident I am using each problem-solving techniques.

Pseudocode: I love this method, it allows me to outline the logic and steps needed to solve a problem without getting caught up in specific syntax. Breaking down the problem into smaller steps through pseudocode enables a more systematic approach, making it easier to tackle and implement each step accurately.



Console logging, reading error messages, googling and trying something: I frequently rely on console logging, error message analysis, and experimentation. As a routine practice, I execute my pseudocode steps within the web browser console, utilizing console.log() to track progress. If an issue arises, the error message precisely identifies the problem's location, prompting me to search for alternative solutions using online resources like Google. This iterative process allows me to explore different approaches and attempt different solutions.



Asking peers and coaches for help:Seeking assistance from peers and coaches is my final resort, as I strive to minimize reliance on external help. I am aware that beyond the course, independent problem-solving becomes crucial. Therefore, I proactively tackle challenges on my own. Only when I encounter prolonged difficulties in seemingly straightforward exercises, I reach out to my facilitator and homegroup for support.



The time I was reluctant to ask for help:

At times, I experience reluctance in seeking help because I have already asked for assistance in previous exercises, and I don't want to impose or burden my facilitator, considering their busy schedule.
